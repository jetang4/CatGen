Mendelian Genetics

Dominance and Recessiveness

How is it possible that two black cats have brown kittens?

In the case of most traits, alleles come in both a dominant and a recessive form. Having two (or more) forms of each trait (alleles) allows for the level of variation we see in everything from hair color to taste certain foods. If you've ever wondered how out of two siblings, one has curly hair and the other straight, dominance and recessiveness can explain it.

Dominant forms of an allele are represented by the CAPITAL form of a letter, and recessive forms are represented by the lowercase form. At each trait locus, you can have two forms of the alleles: both dominant, both recessive, or one of each. By convention, the dominant allele (if any) is written first

Probability

Applying the principles of probability to genetics problems

Probability refers to the likelihood of an event occurring. In the simplest case, we can calculate the probability of an event occurring by dividing the chance of your event of interest occurring by total number of possible events.

For example, what is the probability that a couple's baby is female? We have two possible events: male and female, and one of the two events is 'female'. Thus, we divide 1 by 2, resulting in the probability of having a female being 1/2 or 50%

As you learned in an earlier lesson, traits can be either dominant or recessive, which is how offspring are able to look different than their parents and each other.

Mendelian Genetics

Punnett Squares

Punnett Squares: Probabilities in Genetics can be better understood by the use of punnett squares. In a Cat, the allele for black fur (A) is dominant to the allele for brown color (a). When a parent has the genotype Aa and a father has the genotype Aa, the offspring produce depend on the inheritance of the alleles from both the mother and the father. An easy way to keep track of the probabilities of each is by the use of punnett squares. Watch this short video on punnett squares to proceed to the lecture quiz.
