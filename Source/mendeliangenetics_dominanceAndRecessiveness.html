Dominance and Recessiveness

How is it possible that two black cats have brown kittens?

In the case of most traits, alleles come in both a dominant and a recessive form. Having two (or more) forms of each trait (alleles) allows for the level of variation we see in everything from hair color to taste certain foods. If you've ever wondered how out of two siblings, one has curly hair and the other straight, dominance and recessiveness can explain it.

An organism's genotype for a certain trait is the pair of two alleles they have at that trait's locus (location on a gene). At each trait locus, you can have two forms of the alleles: both dominant, both recessive, or one of each. Dominant forms of an allele are represented by the CAPITAL form of a letter, and recessive forms are represented by the lowercase form. By convention, the dominant allele (if any) is written first

An organism gets one copy of each allele from their mother, and one copy from their father. The allele that is transmitted from the parent to the offspring is completely random. This is how it is possible that the offspring resemble one parent more than the other, or neither parent!
