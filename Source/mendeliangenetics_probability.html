Probability

Applying the principles of probability to genetics problems

All Khan Academy content is available for free at khanacademy.org

Probability refers to the likelihood of an event occurring. In the simplest case, we can calculate the probability of an event occurring by dividing the chance of your event of interest occurring by total number of possible events.

Example: what is the probability that a couple's baby is female? We have two possible events: male and female, and one of the two events is 'female'. Thus, we divide 1 by 2, resulting in the probability of having a female being 1/2 or 50%

As you learned in an earlier lesson, traits can be either dominant or recessive, which is how offspring are able to look different than their parents and each other.

Multiplicative probabilities: when you want to take calculate the probability of an organism having two or more traits, the multiplicative property is used.
Remember that multiplying two fractions results in an even smaller fraction, indicating a lower probability of an event occurring
This makes sense intuitively: the chance of having a female child of any eye color is greater than the chance of having a female child with green eyes specifically.

How do we calculate multiplicative probabilities? Simply take the probability of each trait and multiply them together (multiply the numerators, then multiply the denominators).
