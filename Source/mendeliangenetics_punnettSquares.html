Mendelian Genetics

Punnett Squares

Probabilities in Genetics can be better understood by the use of Punnett squares. In a Cat, the allele for black fur (A) is dominant to the allele for brown colored fur (a). When a parent has the genotype Aa and the father has the genotype Aa, the offspring produced depend on the inheritance of the alleles from both the mother and the father. An easy way to keep track of the probabilities of each is by the use of Punnett squares. Watch this short video on Punnett squares to proceed to the lecture quiz.

The above example illustrates an important distinction. An individual with paring of BB or Bb will both exhibit the dominant trait of brown eyes, even thought the pairing of alleles are different. Here the specific pair of alleles are known as the genotype of the individual. The trait that is displayed is called the phenotype. So for the phenotypes of brown eyes, there are two genotypes. For the phenotypes of blue eyes there is a single genotype. A genotype such as either BB or bb the alleles are identical. An individual with this type of genotype is called homozygous. For a genotype such as Bb the alleles are different from one another. An individual with this type of pairing is called heterozygous.
